# 06 — Playbooks to Learn From

## 8 successful businesses + 10 master patterns + 10 Wendy & Fred actions

Eight businesses that started exactly where Templeman Opticians is now (small, family-run, locally rooted) and became market-defining. What they did, and what you can copy this week.

---

## PLAYBOOK 1 — SPECSAVERS (Doug + Mary Perkins, Guernsey, 1984)

**The story:** Doug and Mary met at Cardiff University in 1962 studying optometry. Built a 24-shop Bristol chain, sold for £2m in 1980, moved to Guernsey, started Specsavers in 1984 from a ping-pong table.

**The key insight:** In 1983 Thatcher's deregulation allowed opticians to advertise for the first time. Most independents refused to advertise. Doug and Mary built their entire business model around it.

**What changed the trajectory:** They invented the **joint venture (JV) model** — the local optometrist becomes 50% owner of their store, takes the local risk, gets a national brand. This solved the "you can't run 1,000 shops from head office" problem. Then **glasses-in-an-hour** in late 1980s. Customers got specs same day. Nobody could match it.

**What Wendy + Fred can borrow:**
1. **You can't fight on price, but you CAN fight on time and intimacy.** Their slot is 20 minutes. Yours can be 45 minutes — and you can advertise that
2. **JV thinking for succession:** offer a young qualified optom a route to part-ownership over 5 years instead of selling to a chain
3. **Embrace local advertising the way they embraced national.** A monthly half-page in *Lincolnshire Free Press* costs less than a stock order of frames

## PLAYBOOK 2 — OPTICAL EXPRESS (David Moulsdale, Glasgow, 1990)

**The story:** Trained as a dispensing optician in the 1980s (same training as Wendy). Founded Optical Express 1990. Now £374m personal wealth (Sunday Times Rich List 2024).

**The key insight:** While Specsavers chased volume specs, David saw that **laser eye surgery would become a high-margin medical service** and that an optical chain on the high street is the perfect funnel. Eye test → consultation → £4,000 procedure.

**What changed the trajectory:** Bought failing small chains cheap (Remocker Shapiro 11 stores in 1995, Specialeyes 65 stores in 1997). Then bought The Health Clinic PLC in 2002 — laser surgery margins kicked in.

**What Wendy + Fred can borrow:**
1. **Be the trusted referrer.** Partner with Optegra / Optical Express / a private cataract surgeon. Refer 1 patient/month, earn a finder's fee
2. **Upsell the medical end.** OCT scans, glaucoma monitoring, dry-eye clinics, myopia management — chains do these poorly. You can do them brilliantly at £40-£80/scan
3. **Bolt-on, don't build from scratch.** A retiring neighbouring practice = cheaper than opening a second shop

## PLAYBOOK 3 — PATAGONIA (Yvon Chouinard, California, 1973)

**The story:** Yvon was a craftsman making climbing gear for friends. Now ~$1.5bn revenue family-owned business that gives 100% of profits (~$100m/year) to environmental charities.

**The key insight:** Customers will pay more — and stay forever — for a brand whose values they can name. Patagonia famously ran a "Don't Buy This Jacket" full-page NYT ad. Sales went UP.

**What changed the trajectory:** 1985 — committed 1% of sales to environment ("1% for the Planet"). 2022 — transferred 100% of voting stock to a purpose trust so values can never be sold off.

**What Wendy + Fred can borrow:**
1. **Write down your "why" on the wall.** Plain English: *"We are the Templemans. We have looked after Lincolnshire's eyes since [year]. We give you the time you deserve, and we keep your records for life."* Print it. Frame it. Put it in the window.
2. **Pick ONE local cause and stick with it for 10 years.** Sight loss charity, dementia friends, local primary school vision screening. Tiny annual commitment. Massive PR moat. Specsavers can't match 10-year local commitment.
3. **Family-run IS the moat.** Put a photo of Wendy + Fred + Nick + the dogs on every leaflet + window. Chains can't fake this.

## PLAYBOOK 4 — STRIPE (Patrick + John Collison, Ireland → SF, 2010)

**The story:** Two Irish brothers built a payments business worth ~$70bn. Started 2010 with seven lines of code.

**The key insight:** All payment companies sold to **finance teams** in long, painful sales meetings. The Collisons noticed the **developer** chose the payment tool — and shipped a product developers loved.

**What changed the trajectory:** "Collison installation" — when an early customer said "we'll try Stripe," Patrick or John would personally walk to the customer's office, sit at their laptop, and install Stripe themselves. No salesperson. Founder did it. Then obsessive investment in reliability + docs + developer experience.

**What Wendy + Fred can borrow:**
1. **Be the founder in the chair.** Wendy or Fred personally greets every new patient + personally hands them their glasses on collection day. Twice. Big impact.
2. **Boring beats flashy.** Same-day fittings. Lifetime free adjustments. Free contact lens consultation. Unglamorous services that lock in 20-year customers.
3. **Your "developer audience" is GPs + care home managers.** Win the local GP + care home manager = won 200 patients/year.

## PLAYBOOK 5 — NOTION (Ivan Zhao, San Francisco, 2013)

**The story:** Productivity software. ~100m users. ~95% organic traffic. No paid marketing for years.

**The key insight:** Give the product away free. Let users build the marketing for you by sharing their templates. Every share is a free advert.

**What changed the trajectory:** Building the Notion Ambassadors programme — superfans hosted local meetups, made YouTube tutorials, answered questions. Notion gave them recognition and pizza money — NOT cash. Authenticity stayed pure.

**What Wendy + Fred can borrow:**
1. **Give one thing away free that compounds.** Free children's eye screening at local primary schools (partly NHS-funded). Every child screened = a 60-year customer.
2. **Make best patients into marketing team.** Print "Templeman Opticians referred by [Patient's Name]" cards. Reward referrals with free lens-clean kit or 10% off. Friends pass cards to friends.
3. **Be visible in local Facebook groups weekly.** "Spalding Community," "Lincolnshire Mums," "Care home managers Lincolnshire" — answer eye questions for free. You become the local authority for free.

## PLAYBOOK 6 — RIVERFORD ORGANICS (Guy Singh-Watson, Devon, 1986)

**The story:** Family farm in Buckfastleigh, Devon. Guy went from 3 acres of organic veg in 1986 to delivering 50,000 organic boxes a week. Converted to 100% employee-owned in 2018-2023.

**The key insight:** Supermarkets squeeze farmers. **Cut out the middleman.** Sell direct to the customer. Charge premium for organic + story + delivery.

**What changed the trajectory:** Built grower co-operative — sceptical local farmers persuaded to go organic and share resources. Then franchising — at peak, 100+ local franchisees ran their own delivery rounds nationwide.

**What Wendy + Fred can borrow:**
1. **The farm is an asset, not just a home.** Nick's research campus idea is right — Riverford monetised the farm by adding pubs, holiday cottages, farm shop. You can do the same: domiciliary fleet HQ, training school for trainee DOs, AI lab tenant rent from Nick's businesses.
2. **Cut out the middleman.** Source frames direct from manufacturers (Norville, Inspecs, or independent European factories) instead of wholesalers. Margin doubles.
3. **Employee Ownership Trust** is a real succession option. If Nick doesn't want to run the practice in 15 years, an EOT (like Riverford) keeps it independent, gives you tax-free exit, rewards loyal staff. Worth a £500 conversation.

## PLAYBOOK 7 — RED HAT (Bob Young + Marc Ewing, North Carolina, 1995)

**The story:** Took FREE open-source Linux and built a $34bn business by selling support, certification, and guarantees on top of it. IBM bought them 2019 for $34bn.

**The key insight:** Customers don't pay for software. They pay for the **peace of mind that someone will pick up the phone at 3am.** ~87% of revenue is subscription support.

**What changed the trajectory:** Realising support subscriptions, not software, were the business. Charged for "we'll guarantee this works for 7 years."

**What Wendy + Fred can borrow:**
1. **Sell a "Templeman Care Plan."** £15/month covers annual eye test + lifetime adjustments + 20% off new frames + priority bookings + free home visit if needed. Recurring revenue. Lifetime customer.
2. **Be the support layer underneath bigger players.** Offer to be **post-surgery aftercare provider** for Optical Express / Optegra laser patients in Lincolnshire. They send patients; you do follow-up scans. Both win.
3. **Sell a guarantee Specsavers can't.** "If your prescription changes within 12 months, we re-glaze for free." Specsavers can't operationalise this at scale. You can.

## PLAYBOOK 8 — CALENDLY (Tope Awotona, Atlanta, 2013) — Bottom-Up SaaS

**The story:** A scheduling tool. $550k seed round to ~$3bn valuation. Profitable in 18 months.

**The key insight:** Build a product that, by its very nature, sends itself to new users. Every Calendly link the recipient sees = a free ad. The product IS the marketing.

**What changed the trajectory:** Doubled down on making sharing frictionless. Self-service signup. Free tier with Calendly logo on every booking page.

**What Wendy + Fred can borrow:**
1. **Every patient leaves with something shareable.** A small fabric microfibre cloth with phone number printed on it. Used daily for 2 years. 700 walking adverts.
2. **Online booking.** Modern booking widget (Setmore/Cliniko/Acuity) means a patient can book at 11pm Sunday from their phone. **30% more bookings overnight.** Single highest-ROI £40/month spend.
3. **Ask every happy patient for Google review on the day.** Hand them a card with QR code. Reviews compound. Specsavers' local branch has 200 reviews; you can have 800.

# 10 MASTER PATTERNS — what every winner did

Reading across all 8 playbooks:

1. **They wrote down WHO their customer was, in plain English.** Not "everyone." Specsavers: "family who couldn't previously afford glasses." Patagonia: "climber who cares about the earth." Riverford: "family who wants to know who grew their food." **Your job: write one sentence answering "who is the Templeman Opticians customer?"**

2. **They gave something away free that was genuinely valuable.** Notion's free tier. Calendly's free booking. Riverford's free recipe cards. Stripe's free docs. Free things attract paying customers.

3. **They had a "founder in the chair" personal touch — for years.** Collisons personally installed Stripe. Yvon Chouinard answered customer letters. Doug Perkins still visits stores. **People buy from people. Especially in Lincolnshire.**

4. **They picked ONE thing they were the BEST at, and said NO to everything else.** Specsavers: cheap and fast. Patagonia: durable and ethical. Stripe: developer-friendly. **You have to pick yours.**

5. **They told a real story — repeated it ruthlessly.** Riverford = "I grew the veg myself." Specsavers = "joint venture with local optometrists." Patagonia = "make less, last longer." **A story you can tell in one sentence is worth £10,000 of advertising.**

6. **They built distribution before they built more product.** All 8 obsessed about how to get to 100, then 1,000, then 10,000 customers — BEFORE adding features.

7. **They turned customers into evangelists.** Notion ambassadors. Riverford box organisers. Specsavers JV partners. **Your most loyal patients are your sales force — if you ask them.**

8. **They were patient — most took 5-10 years to break out.** Riverford founded 1986, didn't go national until early 2000s. Specsavers founded 1984, dominant by 1995. **Internet makes us impatient. Real businesses still take a decade.**

9. **They reinvested almost everything. Almost never took outside money.** Stripe took seed; Patagonia/Specsavers/Riverford took none. **Family-owned beats VC-owned almost every time.**

10. **They had a written succession plan / values lock — early.** Patagonia purpose trust. Specsavers family structure. Riverford EOT. **Without this, even brilliant businesses get sold off.**
