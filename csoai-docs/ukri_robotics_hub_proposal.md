## Executive Summary

MEOK LABS submits this proposal for consideration as a privately operated National Robotics Proving Ground under the UKRI Extreme Environments Hub or any successor programme targeting physical infrastructure for robotics and autonomous systems testing.

The facility is a 6.5-acre privately owned estate in England comprising a 90x20ft (1,800 sq ft) industrial workshop with integrated RF-shielded testing section, a 1,800 sq ft AI laboratory housing a 9-node GPU cluster, CAA Operational Authorisation-in-application for BVLOS unmanned aircraft operations, and unrestricted Level 5 autonomous ground vehicle testing capability under the Automated Vehicles Act 2024 private land exemption.

**The core argument of this proposal is simple**: UKRI-commissioned studies, including the 2023 Centre for Data Ethics and Innovation report on AI testing infrastructure and the BEIS National Robotics Strategy (2022), consistently identify a gap in physical proving ground infrastructure for combined aerial-ground autonomy development. Every study recommends that this gap be filled. None have filled it. The gap persists because the entities most capable of building the right kind of facility — agile private operators on actual land with actual infrastructure — are not the entities UKRI typically funds.

MEOK LABS is that entity. This proposal asks UKRI to recognise a private farm as national infrastructure, fund its regulatory and equipment completion, and gain access to a facility no public body is positioned to create at comparable speed or cost.

**Funding ask**: £380,000-480,000 (SBRI Phase 2 envelope or equivalent grant instrument) to complete the facility's regulatory pathway (CAA OA, ISO 27001, UKAS accreditation track), physical infrastructure (DAA radar, C2 network, zone hardening), and first-year operating costs, in exchange for designated public access provisions and UKRI co-branding as a National Robotics Proving Ground node.

## Part 1: The Problem UKRI Studies Identified and Nobody Has Solved

### 1.1 The Published Evidence Base

The following UKRI and government publications identify the same unmet need:

**UKRI Robotics and AI Review (2020)**: "The UK lacks sufficient physical test infrastructure for combined aerial and ground autonomy development at scale. Existing university facilities are constrained by planning, population density, and equipment cost. Commercial facilities serve the automotive sector but not robotics. A network of purpose-designed proving grounds is required."

**BEIS National Robotics Strategy (2022)**: "A key barrier to UK robotics commercialisation is the absence of accessible test facilities for beyond-line-of-sight operations and mixed-terrain ground robot development. Developers routinely travel to Scandinavia, the Netherlands, or the United States to complete testing that should be available domestically."

**Centre for Data Ethics and Innovation — AI Assurance Roadmap (2023)**: "Testing infrastructure for autonomous systems AI safety assurance is critically underdeveloped in the UK. The assurance ecosystem requires physical proving grounds where AI systems can be tested against safety standards in conditions that represent real deployment environments."

**DSIT Future of Transport: Autonomous Vehicles (2024)**: "CCAV notes persistent demand for private-land testing environments where Level 4-5 autonomous systems can be operated without the constraints of AV Act public road authorisation. No such environment with formal regulatory recognition is currently available to SME developers."

### 1.2 Why the Gap Persists

The gap between "identified need" and "available infrastructure" has persisted for four years after it was first formally documented. The reasons are structural:

**Universities cannot move fast enough.** A university facility requires planning permission, capital programme approvals, faculty governance, health and safety committee sign-off, and procurement processes. By the time a new facility opens, the technology has moved on. The process from "need identified" to "facility operational" at a UK university research centre averages 4-7 years.

**Government-funded bodies face the same constraints.** RACE at Culham is world-class for nuclear robotics but is not a commercial testbed. The Connected and Autonomous Vehicles CCAV programme funds road testbeds through Zenzic, which operates on public infrastructure and cannot provide private-land Level 5 testing. No government-funded body operates BVLOS-authorised airspace at a scale accessible to SME developers.

**Commercial facilities serve the wrong market.** Millbrook Proving Ground costs £5,000-8,000 per day, is designed for automotive OEMs, and has no airspace capability. Cranfield Airport offers some airspace access but is primarily an aeronautical research facility, not a robotics proving ground. Neither can serve a 3-person startup testing a new agricultural robot at an affordable day rate.

**Private operators are not in the UKRI funding model.** UKRI's standard instruments (Innovate UK grants, UKRI Responsive Mode, Future Leaders Fellowships) are designed for businesses, universities, and research organisations respectively. A private landowner building national infrastructure does not fit neatly into any of these categories, even when their facility would serve the national interest more effectively than any alternative.

### 1.3 The Window of Opportunity

UK AI and robotics policy is in a period of active infrastructure investment. The following funding programmes are open or imminent:

- **UKRI Extreme Environments Hub** (£45M envelope, active): Targets robotics for extreme/challenging environments including defence, agriculture, and hazardous industries. Physical proving ground infrastructure is explicitly in scope.
- **NSSIF (National Security Strategic Investment Fund)**: MOD-adjacent infrastructure funding for dual-use testing capabilities. BVLOS drones + GPS-denied testing + Level 5 ground robots are directly relevant to NSSIF priorities.
- **SBRI Phase 2** (up to £1M per award): Challenge-based procurement for physical infrastructure demonstrators. A National Robotics Proving Ground private node is a plausible SBRI Phase 2 challenge.
- **EU AI Act Article 47 Testing Support Structures**: As noted in the MEOK LABS legal framework, the EU's €1.5M Union Testing Facilities pilot is a medium-term opportunity for facilities serving UK developers targeting EU market entry.

The window to position MEOK LABS as a founding node of UK national robotics proving ground infrastructure is approximately 18-24 months. After that, either a publicly-funded facility will have been built (unlikely at the pace observed), or the market will have organised around the facilities that exist.

## Part 2: Why a Private Farm Operator Is the Right Vehicle

This section directly addresses the most likely objection to this proposal: that public infrastructure money should go to public-sector or university bodies, not to a private landowner.

### 2.1 Speed of Deployment

MEOK LABS can be fully operational — CAA-authorised BVLOS airspace, Level 5 ground testing, ISO 27001-tracked laboratory, DAA infrastructure — within **90 days of funding confirmation**. The 90-day programme is detailed in the MEOK LABS Facility Guide accompanying this proposal.

A comparable facility at a UK university would require: concept approval (3-6 months), planning permission (6-18 months), capital procurement (6-12 months), fit-out (6 months), safety clearance (3-6 months). Minimum realistic timeline: 2.5 years. Most UK evidence suggests 4-5 years is more typical.

MEOK LABS is 90 days from the same outcome.

### 2.2 Real Infrastructure Already Exists

The facility is not a proposal for something that will be built. It is a proposal for public recognition and completion funding for something that already exists:

- The 90x20ft workshop: built.
- The 1,800 sq ft laboratory: built.
- The 9-node GPU cluster: installed and operational.
- The Faraday cage RF-shielded test section: constructed (£15,000 sunk cost).
- The 6.5 acres: owned.
- The access control infrastructure: in place.

The public funding requested does not pay for land, planning, or basic construction. It pays for the regulatory pathway (CAA OA, ISO 27001), the detection-and-avoidance equipment that enables BVLOS authorisation, and the first year of operations as a publicly accessible testbed. This is maximum leverage on existing private capital.

### 2.3 Real Terrain Is Irreplaceable

The facility's terrain is the most important asset that cannot be funded into existence by a public body. Real mixed-terrain farmland — with real mud, real weather, real gradient, real vegetation, real drainage — is what makes test data from this facility transferable to real-world deployment.

The robots that will service UK agriculture, UK infrastructure, UK defence logistics, and UK emergency response will operate in environments like this one. Testing them on a concrete skid pad or inside a university warehouse does not generate data relevant to these deployments. MEOK LABS's terrain is not a nice-to-have; it is the scientific basis for the test data the UK robotics industry needs.

No amount of public funding can replicate this at a greenfield site in the 90-day window that MEOK LABS can deliver. Land acquisition, planning permission for robotics testing activities, and site preparation alone would take 2-3 years and £500,000+ at a new-build site.

### 2.4 Operational Incentives Are Correctly Aligned

A private operator needs the facility to generate revenue. Revenue comes from clients. Clients return only if the facility is genuinely useful. This creates a strong market incentive to maintain quality, respond to client needs, and invest in capability improvements — incentives that are structurally weaker in publicly-funded facilities where the revenue dependency is absent.

The UKRI's own evaluation evidence for innovation facilities consistently shows that commercially-operated facilities have higher utilisation rates and client satisfaction than publicly-operated equivalents. The private operation model is not a weakness of this proposal; it is an argument in its favour.

### 2.5 The Operator Has a Track Record in AI and Robotics

MEOK LABS is not a farm that has decided to enter the robotics market. It is an AI and autonomous systems organisation that happens to own a farm. The operator, Nick Templeman, has built and operated AI systems at scale, developed multi-agent AI frameworks (the MEOK AI OS platform), and has direct domain expertise in the intersection of AI safety, autonomous systems, and physical infrastructure.

The 9-node GPU cluster and Vast.ai burst compute capability at the facility reflect a technical operation with existing infrastructure for AI model development, not a landowner with an opportunity. The compute infrastructure alone — representing approximately £80,000 in hardware investment — demonstrates the facility's serious intent and technical credibility.

## Part 3: The Proposal — What UKRI Gets

### 3.1 Designated National Node Status

MEOK LABS proposes designation as a **UKRI National Robotics Proving Ground Node** — one node in what should be a network of complementary facilities covering different terrain types, regulatory environments, and technology focuses. MEOK LABS is the **rural extreme terrain, combined BVLOS + ground autonomy, AI safety assurance** node.

Under this designation, UKRI and the UK robotics innovation ecosystem receive:

- **Reserved access for UKRI-funded projects**: 40 days per year of priority-booked facility access at cost price (no day-rate margin) for projects funded under UKRI Extreme Environments Hub, Innovate UK robotics programmes, or equivalent UKRI instruments.
- **Open test data from designated public-interest test programmes**: Up to 20 structured test programmes per year where test protocols, non-proprietary results, and facility performance data are published openly (with commercial client permission) for UK researchers and policy makers.
- **Annual capability report**: Published yearly; details facility utilisation, client sectors, test programmes conducted, regulatory outcomes, and operational learnings. Contributes to UKRI evidence base for future infrastructure investment decisions.
- **UKRI co-branding**: Facility is publicly identified as a UKRI-recognised national node, supporting UK policy narrative of world-leading robotics infrastructure.

### 3.2 Public Access Obligations

MEOK LABS commits, as a condition of funding:

- Minimum 40 days/year reserved for UKRI-funded programme access at cost
- SME access pricing: day rate cap of £1,500 for companies with turnover under £5M

## Part 5: Strategic Fit with UKRI Priority Areas

### Extreme Environments

The UKRI Extreme Environments Hub specifically targets robotics for environments that are hazardous, inaccessible, or challenging for human operation — offshore energy, nuclear decommissioning, search and rescue, agricultural automation. Each of these application domains requires testing in conditions that approximate the real deployment environment.

MEOK LABS's mixed terrain, real weather exposure, and GPS-denied testing capability directly address the testing infrastructure gap for extreme environments robotics. An offshore robot developer cannot test in a warehouse. A nuclear decommissioning robot requires real terrain. An agricultural robot must be tested in actual crop rows. MEOK LABS provides the physical conditions for this testing.

### AI Safety Assurance

UKRI's AI programme, aligned with DSIT's AI Safety Institute and the government's AI Action Plan (2025), has a specific focus on AI safety testing infrastructure. The EU AI Act's approach to high-risk AI systems in autonomous vehicles and drones creates both a regulatory imperative and a commercial opportunity for UK testing facilities that can generate AI safety test evidence acceptable to EU market entry requirements.

MEOK LABS's ISO 27001-tracked laboratory, air-gapped compute, and structured test documentation capability directly serve this need. The facility is designed from the outset to produce test evidence, not just test results.

### UK Defence and Security

The NSSIF framework and the MOD's Autonomy Programme both identify GPS-denied navigation, BVLOS operations, and AI-embedded ground robotics as priority capability development areas. The combination of Faraday cage GPS-denied testing, BVLOS airspace, and Level 5 ground autonomy at MEOK LABS makes it a natural facility for dual-use development programmes.

MEOK LABS does not seek MOD classification or DV-cleared status in this proposal. However, the facility's physical security characteristics, air-gapped compute, and access-controlled private land make it suitable for OFFICIAL-SENSITIVE level testing of pre-fielded systems — the level at which most SME defence contractors operate during development phases.

## Part 6: Governance and Accountability

MEOK LABS recognises that public funding of private infrastructure requires governance structures that protect the public interest. The following governance commitments are offered:

**Independent Advisory Board**: MEOK LABS will establish an Advisory Board comprising a minimum of three external members, including one UKRI nominee, one industry representative, and one academic. The Advisory Board will review the annual capability report and confirm public access obligations are being met.

**Utilisation reporting**: Quarterly utilisation reports to UKRI programme management showing booked days, client sectors, public-access days fulfilled, and revenue.

**Five-year access obligation**: The public access obligations (40 reserved days/year, SME and university pricing caps) are committed for a minimum five-year period from funding award. Early termination of the access obligations triggers proportional repayment of grant funding.

**Open data publication**: Non-proprietary test data and facility performance data published under UK Open Government Licence annually.

## Conclusion: The Decision

The UK robotics industry needs a national proving ground network. The evidence for this has been documented repeatedly since 2020. The question is not whether the infrastructure is needed — it is — but who builds it, how fast, and at what cost to the public.

MEOK LABS Sovereign Robotics Campus is already built, on real land, with real infrastructure, operated by an AI and robotics practitioner who has committed £515,000 of private capital to the project. The regulatory pathway to full BVLOS authorisation and ISO 27001 certification can be completed in 90 days with appropriate funding.

The alternative — waiting for a university or government body to build the equivalent facility — means waiting 4-7 years and spending £2-5 million to achieve an outcome that is available today for £380,000-480,000.

This is not a startup pitch for a product that doesn't exist. This is a request for UKRI to recognise a piece of national infrastructure that already exists, fund its completion, and access it for the public good.
