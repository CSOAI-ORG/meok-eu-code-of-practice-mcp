## 1. Why a Jersey or Guernsey Trust

Jersey and Guernsey represent the preferred offshore common law jurisdictions for this structure for several reasons.

**Legal framework.** Jersey's Trusts (Jersey) Law 1984, as amended most recently in 2018, provides one of the most sophisticated and codified trust law frameworks in the world. It expressly permits purpose trusts (including mixed purpose/beneficiary trusts), reserved powers for settlors and protectors, and statutory firewall provisions protecting trust assets from foreign forced heirship and creditor claims. Guernsey's Trusts (Guernsey) Law 2007 provides comparable protections.

**English-law compatibility.** Both jurisdictions operate common law systems derived from English equity law. Jersey and Guernsey courts interpret trust concepts in a manner familiar to UK-qualified advisers and courts. This reduces risk when the underlying assets include UK-sited property and UK-registered IP.

**Inheritance tax treatment.** A properly structured offshore discretionary trust can, in certain circumstances, hold assets outside the UK IHT estate of the settlor. This is particularly relevant for non-domiciled or mixed-domicile planning, and for Business Property Relief (BPR) eligible assets. Advice on the settlor's precise domicile position must be obtained.

**Tax status.** Jersey and Guernsey are Crown Dependencies and are not part of the United Kingdom for the purposes of UK domestic tax law, though they are not EU member states and are not offshore secrecy jurisdictions. HMRC has formal exchange-of-information arrangements with both islands. Tax efficiency arises from structure and timing, not opacity.

**Privacy.** Unlike England and Wales, Jersey and Guernsey do not operate public trust registries. The existence and terms of a trust are not publicly available records, though both jurisdictions maintain private regulatory registers accessible to competent authorities. This provides meaningful privacy from commercial counterparties and public search while remaining compliant with OECD transparency standards.

## 2. Structure Recommendation

### Recommended Structure: Reserved Powers Discretionary Trust

A **Reserved Powers Discretionary Trust** under Jersey or Guernsey law is the recommended vehicle. This structure combines the asset-protection benefits of a discretionary trust with explicit statutory protections for the settlor's retained influence over the trust through a formalised Protector role and reserved powers.

**Why not a pure Purpose Trust.** A non-charitable purpose trust (NCPT) under Jersey law is permissible but requires an enforcer and is better suited to single-object vehicles (e.g., a STAR trust holding shares). The MEOK Estate Trust has both human beneficiaries (Nicholas Templeman personally) and a purpose beneficiary (the MEOK LABS research mission), which is better served by a mixed discretionary trust with a defined purpose class.

**Discretionary Trust mechanics.** The trustees hold legal title to trust assets and exercise discretion over distributions to the class of beneficiaries. No beneficiary has a vested interest; all interests are discretionary. This is the core mechanism for creditor protection: because Nick holds no fixed legal or equitable interest in trust assets, a UK judgment creditor cannot compel a distribution or attach trust property, provided the trust is genuine, properly constituted, and the protector powers are not so extensive as to make the trust a sham.

**The Protector role.** Nick should be appointed as **Protector** of the trust rather than trustee. The Protector role is recognised under Jersey Trusts Law 1984, Article 9A (as inserted). The Protector's powers should include:

- Power to remove and appoint trustees (with or without cause)
- Power to veto proposed distributions above a defined threshold
- Power to direct investment of trust assets within defined parameters
- Power to amend the trust deed with trustee consent (restricted amendment power)
- Power to add or remove beneficiaries from the discretionary class

**Caution on reserved powers.** Retained powers must be carefully calibrated. Excessively broad reserved powers risk the trust being treated as a sham or the assets being included in Nick's estate for IHT purposes under the "reservation of benefit" rules (IHTA 1984 s.102). Counsel must review the extent of each reserved power against HMRC's published guidance on settlor-interested trusts.

**Holding UK freehold land through the trust.** UK freehold land cannot be registered at HM Land Registry directly in the name of a foreign trust, as the Land Registration Act 2002 requires a named legal proprietor capable of being registered. The standard mechanism is a **UK nominee company** — a private limited company incorporated in England and Wales, held as a trust asset — which holds the registered legal title to the freehold as nominee/bare trustee for the offshore trust. The trust holds 100% of the shares in the nominee company. This is a well-established and legitimate structure. The nominee company has no independent commercial activity; it exists solely to hold the registered title. Stamp Duty Land Tax (SDLT) implications on transfer into the structure must be separately assessed.

## 3. IP Assignment

The MEOK LABS intellectual property library — comprising 1,010 documents and a suite of proprietary AI evaluation methodologies — is currently owned by MEOK LABS Ltd, a UK private limited company. To place this IP into the trust, MEOK LABS Ltd must formally assign legal and beneficial ownership to the trust vehicle (or a trust-held IP holding company).

**Assignment mechanics.** The assignment requires a written deed of assignment compliant with the Copyright, Designs and Patents Act 1988 and the Patents Act 1977 where applicable. Each category of IP (copyright, database rights, trade secrets, methodologies) must be specifically addressed. An IP schedule listing the 1,010 documents by reference should be attached to the deed; assigning by category description alone carries validity risk.

**Capital Gains Tax on assignment.** A transfer of IP from a UK company (MEOK LABS Ltd) to a connected party (a trust in which Nick is Protector and potential beneficiary) will be treated as a disposal at market value for UK corporation tax purposes under TCGA 1992 s.17. This is not avoidable by undervaluing the IP. Nick must commission an independent IP valuation from a qualified IP valuation firm prior to assignment.

**Rollover relief.** Where the IP constitutes a "qualifying asset" under TCGA 1992 s.152 et seq., and proceeds are reinvested in qualifying replacement assets within the prescribed window, rollover relief may defer the gain. This requires careful structuring and prior HMRC clearance is advisable. The availability of rollover relief in this specific context — assignment to a trust rather than reinvestment in trading assets — must be confirmed by a UK tax counsel specialising in corporate IP transactions. Do not assume rollover relief applies; obtain written confirmation.

## 4. Tax Implications

**Honest assessment — what this structure does not achieve.**

If Nick remains a UK resident and UK domiciliary (or deemed domiciliary under the 15-out-of-20 years rule), a Jersey or Guernsey discretionary trust in which he is a potential beneficiary will be treated as a **settlor-interested trust** for UK income tax purposes. This means:

- All trust income will be taxed on Nick personally as it arises, regardless of whether distributions are made (Income Tax Act 2007 s.624).
- Capital gains realised within the trust are attributed back to Nick under the "transferor of assets abroad" provisions where he retains an interest (TCGA 1992 s.86).

An offshore trust structure does **not** eliminate UK income tax or capital gains tax for a UK-resident, UK-domiciled settlor who remains a potential beneficiary. Counsel and a UK tax adviser must provide a written tax memorandum on Nick's precise domicile and residency position before implementation.

**What this structure does achieve.**

1. **Inheritance Tax (IHT) mitigation.** Assets held in a properly constituted discretionary trust are generally outside the settlor's estate for IHT purposes after seven years from the date of transfer (subject to the gift-with-reservation provisions). The 6.5-acre farm may separately qualify for **Agricultural Property Relief (APR)** at up to 100%, and the IP/equipment may qualify for **Business Property Relief (BPR)** at 100%, if the underlying activity constitutes a qualifying trade. APR and BPR eligibility must be confirmed. Even without relief, the trust structure allows the IHT exposure to be managed across the trust's perpetuity period.

2. **Creditor protection.** Assets transferred to a properly constituted discretionary trust in which Nick holds no fixed entitlement are, in general, not available to his personal creditors, provided the transfer was not made with intent to defraud (Insolvency Act 1986 s.423) and the trust is not a sham.

3. **Succession planning.** The trust provides a clear mechanism for continuation of the MEOK LABS research mission and orderly distribution of assets on Nick's death, without the assets passing through probate.

4. **Separation of personal and business assets.** Holding the farm, equipment, and IP at the trust level rather than personally or in MEOK LABS Ltd creates a clear structural separation, reducing exposure of those assets to MEOK LABS Ltd's operational liabilities.
