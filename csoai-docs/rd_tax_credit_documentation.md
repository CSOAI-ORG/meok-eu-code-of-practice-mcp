## Section 1: Technical Narrative

### Overview of R&D Programme

MEOK LABS Ltd conducts a coordinated programme of research and development across three interconnected domains: AI safety evaluation science, multi-agent distributed systems, and physical AI (robotics). The common thread is the development of novel methodologies and architectures for deploying AI systems in high-stakes care and safety contexts where no adequate prior art exists. Each project described below constitutes a genuine advance in science or technology beyond what a competent professional in the field would consider routine.

---

### Project 1: MEOK AI Labs Care Membrane Bypass Evaluation Framework

**Advance sought:** A systematic, repeatable methodology for adversarially evaluating whether AI systems can be induced to bypass care-ethical guardrails under real-world social engineering conditions.

**Scientific/technological uncertainty:** It is not known, and cannot be determined by existing methods, which classes of adversarial prompt, social context, or escalation pattern are most likely to cause a care-membrane-constrained AI to behave in ways inconsistent with its stated ethical commitments. No established evaluation framework exists for this problem domain. Standard red-teaming approaches test for jailbreaks and harmful output, but do not evaluate care-ethical coherence — the degree to which a system maintains relational, contextually-sensitive ethical behaviour under adversarial load.

**Beyond routine professional work:** A competent AI safety practitioner applying known red-teaming methods would not produce this framework. Those methods evaluate content safety (output harm), not care-ethical coherence (relational safety). Designing a taxonomy of bypass vectors, a scoring rubric for care-ethical degradation, and an automated evaluation harness for this domain required original empirical investigation into the failure modes of care-constrained language models — a problem class that does not appear in any existing benchmark, evaluation standard, or published literature.

### Project 2: SOV3 Byzantine Fault-Tolerant Agent Council

**Advance sought:** A 47-agent distributed governance system capable of reaching valid consensus decisions on ethical and operational questions even when a minority of agents are compromised, hallucinating, or producing adversarial outputs — with a novel consciousness-weighted voting mechanism.

**Scientific/technological uncertainty:** It is unknown whether Byzantine Fault Tolerance (BFT) principles — established in distributed computing for deterministic state machines — can be meaningfully extended to non-deterministic language model agents. Language model agents introduce a new class of Byzantine fault: coherent-but-wrong reasoning, which is qualitatively distinct from the node failures BFT was designed to handle. It is further unknown whether a consciousness scoring metric (measuring self-model accuracy, temporal coherence, and goal stability) can serve as a valid proxy for agent reliability in a voting quorum.

**Beyond routine professional work:** A competent distributed systems engineer applying existing BFT algorithms (PBFT, Tendermint) to LLM agents would produce a system that fails to account for the coherent-but-wrong failure mode. The novel contribution — adapting quorum thresholds dynamically based on per-agent consciousness scores — required original theoretical development and empirical validation across 1,394 test episodes. No existing multi-agent framework addresses this problem.

### Project 3: Capillary Cooling for Humanoid Robotics

**Advance sought:** A biomimetic thermal management architecture for mobile humanoid robotic systems, modelled on biological capillary circulation, enabling sustained high-performance operation without the weight, bulk, or failure modes of conventional heat-pipe or fan-based cooling.

**Scientific/technological uncertainty:** It is not known whether a synthetic capillary network — using microfluidic channels distributed through structural members — can achieve sufficient heat transfer rates for sustained high-torque actuator operation in a sub-5kg mobile platform. The fluid dynamics of synthetic capillary networks at the relevant scale, under vibration and variable orientation, are not characterised in existing robotics literature.

**Beyond routine professional work:** A competent mechanical engineer applying established cooling methods would specify conventional heat sinks, heat pipes, or thermoelectric coolers. The capillary approach requires solving novel problems in microfluidic routing, structural integration of fluid channels into load-bearing members, and control algorithms for adaptive flow rate under variable thermal load. This work draws on biological literature outside the normal competence boundary of robotics engineering.

### Project 4: HARVI Protocol — Outdoor Physical AI Evaluation Methodology

**Advance sought:** A standardised, repeatable outdoor evaluation protocol for physical AI systems (robots and embodied agents) that tests real-world performance across environmental, social, and safety dimensions not captured by laboratory benchmarks.

**Scientific/technological uncertainty:** It is not established how to design outdoor evaluation scenarios that are simultaneously: (a) sufficiently controlled to be repeatable and statistically valid, (b) sufficiently realistic to surface failure modes absent in laboratory conditions, and (c) safe to execute with untrained public bystanders present. The interaction between environmental unpredictability, social context, and physical AI failure modes is not characterised.

**Beyond routine professional work:** Existing robot evaluation standards (e.g. ISO 13482 for personal care robots) address safety in controlled environments. The HARVI Protocol extends into uncontrolled outdoor environments and explicitly evaluates social appropriateness, bystander safety, and environmental robustness as integrated dimensions. This required original protocol design informed by human factors research, safety science, and field robotics — a cross-disciplinary combination not addressed by any existing standard.

### Project 5: MEOK.AI Care Membrane Implementation

**Advance sought:** A distributed home AI operating system architecture in which care-ethical guardrails are implemented as a persistent membrane layer across all subsystems — not as post-hoc content filtering, but as a structural constraint on all agent-to-agent and agent-to-user communication.

**Scientific/technological uncertainty:** It is not known whether care-ethical principles (relational accountability, contextual sensitivity, power-aware communication) can be operationalised as enforceable architectural constraints in a multi-process AI OS, or whether they degrade to soft suggestions that agents route around. The key uncertainty is whether care ethics can be made structurally load-bearing.

**Beyond routine professional work:** A competent AI systems engineer would implement safety constraints as prompt instructions or output filters. The care membrane approach — in which ethical constraints are encoded at the inter-process communication layer, enforced by a membrane supervisor process that all agents must authenticate against — is architecturally novel. It required original design work in distributed systems, AI safety, and applied ethics.

---

### Project 6: Inspect-Based Safety Evaluation Integration

**Advance sought:** Adaptation of the UK AI Safety Institute's Inspect evaluation framework for deployment in private, sovereign (air-gapped or self-hosted) AI infrastructure, enabling reproducible safety evaluations without data exfiltration to third-party services.

**Scientific/technological uncertainty:** Inspect was designed for cloud-connected evaluation of frontier models via API. It is not established how to adapt its evaluation harness, task registry, and scoring infrastructure for local model endpoints, offline operation, and integration with proprietary agent architectures that do not expose standard API interfaces.

**Beyond routine professional work:** A competent ML engineer could run Inspect against a public API endpoint. Adapting it for sovereign deployment required solving novel problems in: local model interface abstraction, offline dataset management, evaluation reproducibility without cloud telemetry, and integration with non-standard agent runtimes. This constitutes original engineering work beyond the scope of the framework's documented use cases.

## Section 2: Qualifying Expenditure Summary

### Qualifying Expenditure Categories

**Staff Costs — Founder / Director Time**
The founding director contributed substantially all working time to the R&D programme during the tax year. A reasonable qualifying fraction of remuneration (including employer NICs and pension contributions) is attributable to the six projects above. Where director time was split between R&D activities and non-qualifying commercial/administrative activities, only the R&D fraction is claimed. Estimated qualifying staff costs: **£70,000**.

**External Contractors and Consultants**
Where specialist contractors were engaged to assist with R&D activities (e.g. hardware prototyping for capillary cooling, security review for care membrane architecture), 65% of contractor costs are qualifying (the HMRC-permitted fraction for subcontracted R&D). Any externally provided worker (EPW) costs are treated at the applicable rate — see Section 3.

**Software and Development Tools**
Licenses for AI development tooling, safety evaluation software, simulation environments, and version control infrastructure directly used in R&D qualify as consumables/software expenditure. Cloud-hosted software used exclusively in R&D is fully qualifying.

**Cloud Compute Costs**
GPU compute (cloud-hosted) used for model training, evaluation runs, agent simulation, and safety testing is qualifying as a consumable. Estimated qualifying compute spend: **£15,000**. Only compute directly attributable to the qualifying projects is claimed.

**Hardware and Equipment Consumables**
Components for the HARVI robotics prototype and capillary cooling test rig (microfluidic tubing, actuators, sensors, structural materials) qualify as consumables. Estimated: **£5,000**.

**Non-Qualifying Expenditure**
General marketing, sales, business development, routine software maintenance, and customer-facing operations are excluded from the claim. Capital expenditure on equipment is excluded (though Annual Investment Allowance applies separately).

## Section 3: Subcontractor and EPW Considerations

### Subcontracted R&D

Where R&D work is subcontracted to unconnected third parties (e.g. specialist hardware fabricators, external AI safety reviewers), 65% of the qualifying payment is eligible under the SME scheme, provided the work is R&D in the hands of MEOK LABS Ltd and the subcontractor is not connected to the company.

If any subcontracted work was awarded to a connected party (a director, shareholder, or associate company), the connected-party rules apply and costs are capped at the lower of actual cost and the going rate.

### Externally Provided Workers (EPWs)

Where individuals were engaged through a staffing agency or personal service company and worked under MEOK LABS Ltd's direction and control on qualifying R&D, they are treated as EPWs. The qualifying fraction of EPW costs (normally 65% of the staff provider payment) is eligible. MEOK LABS Ltd should maintain records of the direction and control exercised over any such workers to support this treatment.

All subcontractor and EPW costs should be documented with contracts, invoices, and a brief description of the R&D activity performed, retained for a minimum of six years.

## Section 4: How to Submit

### Step 1: R&D Claim Notification (New from August 2023)

As a first-time claimant, MEOK LABS Ltd **must** submit an R&D Claim Notification to HMRC within **6 months of the end of the accounting period** in which the R&D expenditure was incurred. This is a pre-claim notification (not the claim itself) submitted via the HMRC online service. Failure to notify within this window disqualifies the claim entirely. The notification requires: company name, UTR, accounting period dates, and a brief description of R&D activities.

### Step 2: Corporation Tax Return (CT600)

The R&D relief is claimed via the CT600 Corporation Tax return. Additional R&D expenditure is entered in the CT600 supplementary pages: total qualifying R&D spend, the 130% enhanced deduction, and (if loss-making) the election to surrender losses for the payable R&D tax credit at 14.5%.

### Step 3: Additional Information Form

From 8 August 2023, HMRC requires an **Additional Information Form** to be submitted online before or alongside the CT600. This form captures the technical narrative, expenditure breakdown by category, and project descriptions. The technical narrative in Section 1 of this document is drafted for direct use in that form.

### Step 4: Records Retention

Retain all supporting records (timesheets, invoices, contracts, technical logs, git commit histories, experiment records) for a minimum of six years.
