## 3. Legal Feasibility — Global Framework Assessment (~2000 words, 1 table)
### 3.1 Blockchain Evidence Recognition
#### 3.1.1 China: Hangzhou Internet Court June 2018 — first globally to accept blockchain evidence; three dedicated judicial blockchain platforms operational
#### 3.1.2 EU: eIDAS Regulation 910/2014 — qualified electronic timestamps have legal presumption of accuracy across 27 member states; eIDAS 2.0 (2024) explicitly integrates blockchain
#### 3.1.3 US: FRE 902(13)(14) amended 2017 — self-authenticating electronic records; Vermont/Arizona/Illinois/Delaware blockchain legislation
#### 3.1.4 France: March 2025 Tribunal Judiciaire de Marseille — blockchain timestamping recognized as valid proof of copyright anteriority; European precedent
#### 3.1.5 WIPO: Explicitly recognizes blockchain as valid measure to prove prior authorship of creation
### 3.2 Patent Law Compatibility
#### 3.2.1 35 U.S.C. § 102/273: Prior user rights + first-to-file framework — defensive publication creates valid prior art if publicly accessible
#### 3.2.2 Article 54(2) EPC: Anything made available to public before filing date is prior art — blockchain disclosure qualifies if publicly accessible
#### 3.2.3 Article 55 EPC: 6-month grace period for disclosures — blockchain timestamp provides independent proof of disclosure date
#### 3.2.4 CSOAI Crosswalk Engine: Patent law coverage across US/EU/UK/JP/CN jurisdictions enables multi-jurisdiction defensive publication
### 3.3 Regulatory Risk Assessment
#### 3.3.1 Low risk: Defensive publication is legal in all jurisdictions; no regulatory license required
#### 3.3.2 Medium risk: AI-generated patent claims may face inventorship challenges (Thaler v. Vidal precedent — AI cannot be inventor, but AI-assisted human invention is patentable)
#### 3.3.3 Mitigation: Always attribute invention to human inventor; AI is a tool, not an inventor
